The nine planetary boundaries

A safe operating space for humanity

Defined by the Stockholm Resilience Centre, these nine processes regulate the stability of the planet. Staying within them keeps Earth in the favourable state that allowed societies to develop.

Climate change

The build-up of greenhouse gases that warms the planet and destabilises the climate system. Driven mainly by burning fossil fuels.

Biodiversity (biosphere integrity)

The loss of species and the erosion of ecosystems. Healthy, diverse life keeps natural systems resilient and functioning.

Land system change

The conversion of forests, wetlands, and grasslands into farmland and cities, which weakens the land's ability to support life and store carbon.

Freshwater use

The disruption of the water cycle through over-extraction of rivers, lakes, and groundwater, and changes to soil moisture.

Biogeochemical flows

The overloading of nitrogen and phosphorus, mostly from fertilisers, which pollutes water and triggers algal blooms and dead zones.

Ocean acidification

As oceans absorb excess CO2 they become more acidic, making it harder for corals and shellfish to build their skeletons and shells.

Atmospheric aerosols

Tiny particles from pollution that harm health and alter rainfall patterns and regional climates.

Stratospheric ozone depletion

The thinning of the ozone layer that shields life from harmful ultraviolet radiation. Recovering thanks to global action on CFCs.

Novel entities

Human-made substances new to nature, plastics, synthetic chemicals, heavy metals, that ecosystems never evolved to cope with.
