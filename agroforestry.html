Coto Brus, Costa Rica

Coffee agroforestry in Costa Rica

Smallholder coffee farmers in Coto Brus are growing their crop under shade trees again, rebuilding both the soil and the business of farming at the same time.

← Back to Living Proof

Coto Brus, in southern Costa Rica, is coffee country: around 4,000 farmers, most working less than five hectares each, with the local economy and thousands of seasonal workers from Panama's Ngäbe-Buglé indigenous communities depending on the crop. But coffee production in the region had fallen by roughly 50% over fifteen years, squeezed from two directions at once: a changing climate bringing fungal disease and erratic flowering, and a global coffee price that, in 2018, dropped below what most smallholders need just to break even.

A small group of ten farmers in Coto Brus, calling themselves Exportaciones Aromas Coffee, decided incremental fixes weren't enough. Led by 44-year-old farmer Armando Navarro, they built a shared coffee processing plant, the first of its kind run by an independent smallholder group in Costa Rica, so each farmer could process and trace their own beans rather than selling anonymously through a cooperative. At the same time, many in the group returned to growing coffee under a shaded canopy of mixed trees, certifying their farms through Costa Rica's national Bandera Azul Ecológica programme, which rewards shade cover for the biodiversity, erosion control, nitrogen fixing, and water protection it provides.

What changed

The new processing plant let farmers negotiate directly with exporters and buyers instead of going through middlemen, capturing more of the value of their own harvest. A partnership with the exporter Cafinter introduced drip irrigation to save water and gave farmers access to new, more climate-resilient coffee varieties. Collective bargaining for fertilizer and other inputs helped keep production costs down as global prices stayed volatile. And the shift back to shade-grown systems, the same approach long-term research trials in Costa Rica have studied since 2000, restored some of the ecosystem services, soil protection, pest regulation, water retention, that decades of full-sun monoculture had stripped away.

Why it matters

Coto Brus shows that climate adaptation and economic survival aren't separate problems for smallholder farmers, they're the same problem. By rebuilding shade cover, securing a stake in their own processing and sales, and organising collectively, these farmers turned an existential threat to their livelihoods into a working model that other coffee-growing communities in Costa Rica are now starting to study and copy.
